TEs in 2022

TL;DR

  • It's Kelce's world and we are all living in it!
  • Kittle is in his own tier, below Kelce, above the rest
  • Ertz has surprising value for his production
  • Goedert is the next best TE after Kelce, Kittle, and the Hock, Andrews, Ertz tier
  • I want out on Kmet

Overview

This post continues my 2022 player performance review by position group. This edition focuses on tight ends. Again, I hope to explore stats from last season to try and find valuable insights about player value.

To see my previous work on quarterbacks, check out this page. Check this page out for my analysis on wide receivers.

A disclaimer about the data I will be looking over in this post:

  • I don't take into account anything related to two point conversions, partly due to the randomness and small sample size but mostly because it was difficult to incorporate into the data I fetched
  • The top 24 TEs I analyze are by the top 24 fantasy point totals, this is done for simplicity and reusability of graphing code

Fantasy Points (Standard)

Let us start, as always, by taking a gander at fantasy points over the course of a season and on a points per game basis.

My takeaway from these two graphs is that Kelce is positional dominance personified. There is no one close to him, at all! Kelce had 206 total points in standard last year, while second place Kittle had 141 total points. That is a 65 point difference!

On a per game basis, Kittle does close the gap a little bit. Kelce had 12.11 points per game. Kittle had 9.4 points per game. That is a difference of 2.71 points per game.

Mind you, there's a big gap here between both of these guys and the TE1 threshold, which was Juwan Johnson, with 5.81 points per game. Kelce outscores this threshold by 6.3 points per game. Kittle outscores the TE1 threshold by 3.59 points per game.

Surprise, surprise, tight end is a desolate wasteland.

I would also say that based on these numbers, Andrews and Hockenson don't really justify their value. Andrews scored 7.86 points per game last year, while Hockenson scored even less with 7.58 points per game. For what you have to pay to get these guys, I'm not sure their price matches the positional advantage they offer, which is lower than I expected.

One last thing I want to note before moving onto evaluating fantasy points in PPR: Ertz seems to produce above his value. He had 6.9 points per game last year and he is currently (10/3/23) valued as TE31 on KTC. That is some serious value, compared to Hock and Andrews, who are TE3 and TE4, respectively.

Fantasy Points (PPR)

Let us continue examining fantasy points, this time in PPR scoring.

Overall, the trends stay pretty similar in PPR. Kelce is still in a world of his own, although he has increased the distance between himself and the rest. He outscores the TE2 on the season, Hock, by 100.9 points. Kelce also outscores the TE2 on a per game basis, Kittle, by 5.24 points per game. Kelce also outscores the TE1 threshold by 9.55 points per game!

ELITE!

Additionally, Kittle keeps his rank of TE2 on a per game basis at 13.36 points per game. Hockenson and Andrews do close the gap here, scoring 12.67 and 12.7 points per game, respectively. Again, are they worth their value? I'm not so sure... but they are most definitely better bang for your buck in PPR.

Lastly, I want to point out Ertz again. He scored 11.55 points per game in 2022, good enough to be TE6 when looking at points scored per game. I think there is great value to be had there.

Before moving onto receiving yards, I wanted to show this graph just to highlight the difference between Kelce and those other "good" tight ends. Wow...

Other Receiving Stats

Let's move onto some other receiving stats, starting with yards per catch:

Similar to wide receivers, yards per catch doesn't seem to really correlate with fantasy performance.

Although, all of Kelce, Kittle, Andrews, and Hock had more than 10 yards per catch, so perhaps there is a minimum threshold that correlates to fantasy performance, albeit a low one.

Similar to wide receivers, there is a strong correlation between a tight end's volume (perceived and realized) and their fantasy performance.

Kelce's dominance stands out again: he is the only tight end who surpasses the golden 8 targets per game to 6 catches per game ratio (see my wide receiver post for more on this). He had 8.94 targets a game and 6.47 catches a game.

The other thing that stood out to me here was how low Kmet's perceived (4.05 targets a game) and realized (2.94 catches a game) volume was. That's pretty unsustainable, and yet another Bear that terrifies me when I dive into the numbers.

The last item I want to point out from here is how close Ertz is to Andrews and Hock on the per game numbers. Ertz had 6.9 targets a game and 4.7 catches a game. Andrews had 7.53 targets a game and 4.86 catches a game. Hock had 7.58 targets a game and 5.05 catches a game.

The more I look into the per game numbers, the more of a steal Ertz appears to me.

Tiers (Season Totals)

If you've been following my work, you'll notice this section is new. I've spent some time upgrading my python code base and found clustering to be the next step in progressing my tooling.

For those unfamiliar with clustering, it is a type of machine learning technique used to group similar data points together based on certain features, without having prior labels for the groups.

To provide something more tangible, imagine you have a big box of various fruits mixed together: apples, bananas, and grapes. Now, you're asked to sort these fruits into separate baskets without being told what the categories are.

Clustering in machine learning is a bit like sorting these fruits.

The specific version of clustering that I use is called K-Means clustering, which partitions the data into k clusters. In reference to the fruit example, k would specify the number of baskets you would have to sort the fruit.

As a result, I won't be commenting much on the actual numbers here, but rather the clusters, or tiers that are created, as the algorithm handles the comparision of numbers for us.

Summary

Here's my quick recap:

  • It's Kelce's world and we are all living in it!
  • Kittle in his own tier, below Kelce
  • Ertz has surprising value for his production
  • Goedert is the next best TE after Kelce, Kittle, Hock/Andrews/Ertz
  • Kmet is a trap!

Criteria you are looking for in a potential (quality) TE1:

  • 700+ receiving yards
  • 65+ receptions (close to at least 4 catches a game)
  • 80+ targets (close to at least 6)
  • 4+ touchdowns
