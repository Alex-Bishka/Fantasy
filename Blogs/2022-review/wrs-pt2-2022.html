WRs in 2022 Part 2

TL;DR

  • We are in a golden age of wide receiver talent!
  • London is a great buy low. Has been a WR3 with some of the worst quarterback play in the NFL. It can only go up from here (I hope).
  • Add Christian Watson to the list of buy lows. He flashed quite some ability in 2022, but injuries and quarterback concerns have depressed his value.
  • There's a lot of good young (Wilson, Olave, Pickens, Watson, London, etc.) and old talent (Hopkins, Evans, Allen, etc.) for both rebuilders and contenders to buy.
  • Plenty of studs at varying ages prices as well: Chase, Jefferson, Lamb, Brown, Adams, Diggs, Hill, etc.
  • Maybe Chase shouldn't be in the same tier as Jefferson? Yes, he has one of the most insane ceilings in fantasy, but his consistency is not quite at the same level as that of Jefferson. It seems Burrow is a big factor in keeping these two close in value, but does Dobbs change that?

Overview

Welcome back to the final installment of the 2022 position group analysis series. This last piece will continue upon the first 2022 wide receiver analysis piece. This time around, we will be using receiving stats - season totals and per game numbers - to create wide receiver tiers.

I will only be looking at receiving stats for wide receivers, as so few of them actually get carries and produce off of carries. Outside of Amon-Ra, Curtis Samuel, and Deebo, no one really produced on the ground last year. Something to keep in mind for those three, but largely discard for the rest of the wide receivers we will be taking a look at.

As always, our goal is to find players who are undervalued so that we can acquire them and acrew more value on our rosters.

My data disclaimer, as always:

  • I don't take into account anything related to two point conversions, partly due to the randomness and small sample size but mostly because it was difficult to incorporate into the data I fetched
  • The top 48 WRs I analyze are by the top 48 fantasy point totals, this is done for simplicity and reusability of graphing code

For those who wish to explore other analysis pieces, please check out the blogs page on the site.

For the most part, these tiers seem pretty reflective of expectations. There are some players who are in far lower tiers, respective to their quality, but this is largely due to injuries.

Players like Kupp, Chase, Deebo, Keenan Allen and Mike Williams drop a bit further that you would expect in these rankings, but each of these guys had injury issues last year. Mike and Deebo played the most of these four, with 13 games each.

Marquise Brown, Hopkins, DJ Moore are some other names in Cluster 0 that also deserve a mention for finishing in a tier lower than you might expect. The first two were capped to 10 games or less due to injury issues and suspensions, respectively. Moore simply had abysmal quarterback play in Carolina last season.

Outside of these fallers, the one, perhaps surprising, riser would be Zay Jones in my eyes. He had a career year last season, which led to near WR2 production. I don't think there was a strong inclination to buy and capitablize on this performance, as Ridley was coming back for the Jags.

While the rest of the clusters are about what you'd expect, there still is intrigue in each of them.

In cluster 0 and 2, there is a lot of young talent. London, Pickens, Watson, Olave, and Wilson headline the young group of talented receivers. I would buy any where I can, as I believe they will all get better. London, Pickens and Watson are probably the best value buys for me at the moment, as they don't quite have the value or hype of Olave or Wilson, but I think they have shown flashes of playing at an elite level.

Additionally, you have proven, young, studs like Amon-Ra, the Slim Reaper, and Waddle who could all break into Cluster 1, sooner or later (it seems the Sun God is definitely taking the sooner approach with his 2023 season thus far).

The change with six tiers comes from the worst cluster in the previous graph being split into two: Cluster 0 and Cluster 4. Essentially, the split occurs from the disparity in touchdown numbers between the two groups, as the rest of their stats are quite similar. Everyone in Cluster 0 had at least five touchdowns last season, whereas everyone in Cluster 4 had four or less touchdowns. The rest of the picture stays more or less the same.

Now, from an individual perspective, there are a few points I'd like to mention. First, Olave and London being in Cluster 4 is not that bad. That's still WR3 territory. Sure, you would want more production in the endzone, but the targets and yardage numbers are extremely encourging.

Olave had 72 receptions on 119 targets for 1042 yards last season. That's a really strong rookie season, especially since Andy Dalton started 14 games. Now, is Derek Carr better for Olave? That's tough to say - it seemed so, until Kamara came back from his suspension. That being said, Olave is young and is producing even more this year - breaking into WR2 territory - so I do like his future, and would pay up for him. That being said, we might not see him reach his ceiling until the Saints get a better quarterback, or are more willing to throw it deep with Carr.

The story is quite similar for London. He had a wonderful rookie season - 72 receptions on 117 targets for 866 yards - despite awful quarterback play from Mariota and Ridder. London will probably end up with similar numbers to last season, which isn't ideal, but between Ridder and Heinicke, the quarterback play hasn't improved. In fact, it's probably worse this year, as Mariota had a QBR of 57.7 in 2022 and Ridder has a QBR of 39.4 through his nine games this year. This makes London a great buy for me, because his value is severely deflated from horrible quarterback play, and there's not much room for the quarterbacks in Atlanta to get worse.

Outside of those two young bucks, CeeDee Lamb being in Cluster 1 with Adams, Diggs, and Brown is very encourging to me. I believe that, in addition to his performances this year (top 5 wide receiver in PPR and half-PPR formats), CeeDee has solidified himself in that group of top receivers below Chase and Jefferson.

In terms of vets that offer good value for their production, I would look to Lockett, Evans, and Cooper from Cluster 3 and Godwin and Diontae in Cluster 2. All of them continue to provide, at least, WR3 production when healthy. Note, the highest valued player out of this group of vets on KTC (as of 10/20/22) is Evans, at WR32. That's really good value for any of them.

I think a lot more can be said, with how deep and exciting the wide receiver position is, but I'll leave it at that for the six tier analysis.

Some shifts do occur in this final tiering, but here are some final points:

Ridley coming back has hurt his production, but the injuries have really derailed Jones this year. That being said, he's still a critical piece for opening up the Jags offense, just not really an important fantasy piece anymore.
  • It's good to see Chase in that top tier on a per game basis. Injuries derailed his totals last year.

Summary

As always, my recap/takeaways:

  • We are in a golden age of wide receiver talent!
  • London is a great buy low. Has been a WR3 with some of the worst quarterback play in the NFL. It can only go up from here (I hope).
  • Add Christian Watson to the list of buy lows. He flashed quite some ability in 2022, but injuries and quarterback concerns have depressed his value.
  • There's a lot of good young (Wilson, Olave, Pickens, Watson, London, etc.) and old talent (Hopkins, Evans, Allen, etc.) for both rebuilders and contenders to buy.
  • Plenty of studs at varying ages prices as well: Chase, Jefferson, Lamb, Brown, Adams, Diggs, Hill, etc.
  • Maybe Chase shouldn't be in the same tier as Jefferson? Yes, he has one of the most insane ceilings in fantasy, but his consistency is not quite at the same level as that of Jefferson. It seems Burrow is a big factor in keeping these two close in value, but does Dobbs change that?
