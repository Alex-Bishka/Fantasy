RBs in 2022 Part 3

TL;DR

  • Contender buy considerations: CMC, Ekeler, Kamara, Conner, Swift, Monty, Mostert and Jones.
  • Cornerstone buy considerations: Taylor, ETN, K9, and Pacheco(?).
  • Players to consider dumping: Mixon, White, and Pollard.
  • Buy low considerations: Pierce, Taylor, Conner, and Jones.

Overview

This is the last piece on running back performances in 2022. See parts one and two for the complete picture.

We will be focusing on trying to create tiers of backs - via clustering - based on their all around game.

In future articles, we may revisit the quarterback and wide receiver positions to try and gain insights via clustering and tiers.

If you would like to re-read a previous piece, or want to see the previous analysis, please check out the blogs page.

My data disclaimer, as always:

  • I don't take into account anything related to two point conversions, partly due to the randomness and small sample size but mostly because it was difficult to incorporate into the data I fetched
  • The top 40 RBs I analyze are by the top 40 fantasy point totals, this is done for simplicity and reusability of graphing code

Backs that concern me from being in Cluster 0: Mixon and White.

His issues seem similar to Najee's for me: they just are a little slow, and you can't help but feel a more explosive back would be better than either of them in their current situations. Breaking down each tier:

  • Cluster 1: Slightly better than Cluster 0 at everything except targets and receptions.
  • Cluster 2: CMC and Ekeler - the monsters who specialize in the receiving game.

There are no changes to that bottom tier, so no additional comments for Cluster 0.

I am a little disappointed in Pollard and K9 being in Cluster 1. These are guys, who for their price, you would want to see them probably be in Cluster 3.

I am a bigger fan of K9 because of his home run ability. Also, K9's numbers are a little skewed last year, as he didn't play much weeks 1-4 last year, and missed week 13. As a result, I'm willing to give him more of a pass than Pollard.

Pollard to me was a trap for this year. In redraft, he was going around round 2, which feels like his ceiling to me. I'm skeptical that he can have a better year than last, which was buoyed by 12 touchdowns. Of the backs to get 12 or more touchdowns, only Elliot and Jamaal Williams had less total yardage, and while Pollard's total yardage - 1378 - is not bad, it is lower than every other back in Cluster 2, except Najee.

This means there were 11 backs with more total yards than him. Additionally, there's a handful of backs in his neighborhood. As a result, he has strong competition to finish higher in fantasy than last season. For that reason, I felt he was being drafted and valued at his ceiling. I think Pollard is a great player and a solid fantasy asset, but I hated his price before the season (he was RB10 to RB5 on KTC from Aug. 16 to Sept. 26).

Pollard might make an intersting buy low at the end of the season, if he finishes as an RB2/fringe RB1.

The other big point to talk about is Najee in Cluster 3. There are some really good fantasy seasons in that Cluster, so it is a little surprising seeing him there at first, but Najee finished strong last season to end on 1263 yards and 10 touchdowns.

That's not a bad season by any means, but you probably wouldn't expect Najee's name to be paired with the rest of those backs. He is an outlier in that group, but the fumble, touchdown and carry numbers just squeeze him into it.

A quick rundown of the clusters:

  • Cluster 0: Touchdown hogs with a decent number of carries. Pretty mediocre at everything else.
  • Cluster 1: Backs with a solid all around game, but don't score as much as you would like.
  • Cluster 2: Reliable touchdown and yardage numbers is an understatement. You wish their receptions and targets could be just as dependable. Decent efficiency numbers.
  • Cluster 3: Probably the most efficient cluster of backs. Middling for yardage and scoring, and even worse with receptions and targets. Low-to-middle of the pack for carries as well.
  • Cluster 4: Running back royalty: CMC and Ekeler.
  • Cluster 5: Bad at just about everything. These backs redeem themselves slightly with their receptions and targets.

Our bottom tier, represented by Cluster 5 here, remains unchanged.

We still have passing royalty in Cluster 4 and rushing royalty in Cluster 2. Cluster 2 has shrunk to basically the backs who had elite rushing numbers last year, plus Dalvin Cook. I say "plus Cook" as he is towards the bottom of this group in touchdowns scored and total rushing yards. Cook's receptions and targets just get him into this tier.

Cluster 0 is something worth pointing out, as there usually are a couple of backs who end up being redzone monsters. Elliot and Williams held that role last year, but having an idea of who might spring into that role in upcoming seasons would be very valuable, as these types of backs can be bought for cheap and lead a championship charge for you.

Our remaining two clusters have quality backs in them, but I am slightly more partial to Cluster 1 as they seem to get more volume (both in the passing and running game) and accumulate more yardage.

It should be noted that I am staunchly of the philosophy that yardage is most import stat to a player, as I believe it to be far more replicable than touchdown numbers. Perhaps I will be adding an analysis piece on this in the near future.

  • Cluster 1: Elite yardage and scoring numbers, on decent running efficiency. Middle of the pack for targets and receptions. The rushing version of the CMC/Ekeler tier.
  • Cluster 2: Meh-to-poor yardage numbers, with middle of the pack touchdown numbers. On the heavier side of targets and receptions. Not many carries, and all over the place with the rushing efficiency. Don't really fumble.

  • Cluster 4: PPR Kings CMC and Ekeler.
  • Cluster 5: Backs that are good at everything except scoring. They also tend to fumble a bit more than the other clusters.
  • Cluster 6: Great touchdown numbers on okay yardage. Less than ideal in the receiving game. Decent amount of carries, on varying efficiency.

Our worst tier has finally been split in two: Cluster 2 and Cluster 3.

Cluster 2 is actually fantasy relevant. All of these backs were saved by their receiving work. Note, Swift and Mixon are here. I am more optimistic on Swift, because he has improved his situation a lot more and Mixon's problems seem realted to age.

Cluster 3 is abysmal. These backs might serve as a spot start here and there, but should generally be out of your lineup. White might be the exception to the rule, as his 2023 season portrays him as Big Lenny lite, which is to say solid. Again, I do have concerns with White. He reminds me of Najee a lot when I see him: a plodder.

Clusters 1 and 4 are the refined, regal ones for rushing and receiving, respectively. Outside of Chubb (RIP), I would be happy with any of these backs on a contending roster.

Cluster 0 is full of RB3s. Outside of Conner - who is significantly better on a per game basis - and Taylor - as a potential buy low - I did not want to touch any of the backs in this tier.

However, with how things have gone this year, Mostert is worth paying for as a contender. I would not break the bank for him due to age and injury concerns, but his 12 touchdowns this year is insane!

Clusters 5 and 6 consists of player seasons that fringe RB1s, or solidly RB2. These are the types of guys who I look at for trying to find good value, especially if the situation improves, or if someone who is better takes over their situation (see Monty and Swift replacing Williams and Sanders).

I would say that K9 in Cluster 6 might represent an opportunity to buy low on him, especially since his last few games have been a bit undesirable. K9 also has a tough remaining schedule, so there might be a better chance to buy him. There is a little risk with buying K9 with Charbonnet in that backfield, but his home run ability is soooo special. I see K9 as, at worst, a perennial RB2.

That does it for the season total analysis.

A quick rundown of each cluster:

  • Cluster 0: Mixon and Big Lenny: average yards and tds per game, but a strong presence in the passing game.
  • Cluster 1: Jacobs and Henery: yardage monsters who can get into the endzone reliably. Some presence in the passing game, but their forte is on the ground.
  • Cluster 2: Another strong rushing group of backs. Less prolific yardage numbers, but stronger scoring numbers than Cluseter 1. Slightly less presence in the passing game than Cluster 1, but a bit more efficient and safer with the ball.
  • Cluster 3: Good all around backs who just need to get into the endzone a bit more to be monsters. A bit of a fumbling problem.
  • Cluster 4: Ekeler and CMC: the Gods of PPR.
  • Cluster 5: Pretty poor, as group, at everything exception receptions and targets per game.
  • Cluster 6: A slightly worse version of Cluster 2.

The picture largely stays the same, but we see that Mixon and Big Lenny are a budget version of Ekeler and CMC. Still produce good value last year.

We are also shown the distinction between an elite, optimizied rushing back in Cluster 1 and those who are close, but not quite there in Cluster 2 (a bit harsh on Chubb in my option). Again, great to see K9 in that cluster with Chubb.

Our poor man's Mixon and Big Lenny tier, remains with Cluster 5. The numbers continue to hate on White, eh? In his defense, his role was behind Big Lenny, so take it with some salt.

Cluster 3 is the sweet spot for some good buys for contenders looking to get affordable vets (Kamara, Conner, etc.) and those looking to grab cornerstones to finish a rebuild (ETN and Taylor).

The last cluster to mention is six. We've already covered Swift, but two big surprises have come from that cluster this year: Pacheco and Mostert.

Mostert has looked electric in that Miami offense, and his touchdown numbers are absolutely wild this year (12 total already).

Pacheco on the other hand, seems to have stolen a lot of McKinnon's role, which is super exciting as his big weakness last year was his lack of involvement in the passing game. He was a fringe RB3 last year, despite a very slow start (he barely played weeks 1-7). Throw in the extra games and a good amount of McKinnon's receiving production and you have a strong candidate for an RB1.

Pacheco might very well be the budget version of ETN. An interesting buy for both contenders and rebuilders who are close to being relevant.

  • Cornerstone buy considerations: Taylor, ETN, K9, and Pacheco(?).
  • Players to consider dumping: Mixon, White, and Pollard.
